In the first post of this series we built a small electricity-only dispatch in C++/CPLEX. The second post added the heat sector: CHPs, a heat-only boiler, a heat tank, and a district-heat trading link, with the CHP heat-to-power coupling as the only inter-carrier constraint. The third post added a fleet of plug-in electric vehicles modelled as time-windowed flexible storage on the electric bus.

The model still ignores one carrier that is, in many real installations, on the same order of magnitude as heat: cooling. This post adds it, and in doing so finally closes the loop between the three carriers.

Recap and what changes

The new ingredients are minimal in number but rich in coupling:

  • An absorption chiller (AC) that consumes heat and produces cooling with a coefficient of performance $\text{COP}_{\text{ac}}$.
  • An electric chiller (EC) that consumes electricity and produces cooling with a coefficient of performance $\text{COP}_{\text{ec}}$.
  • A new hourly cooling demand $C_{\text{load}}(t)$ and a third balance equation that closes the cooling sector.

With these in place every kWh of CHP electricity has a heat shadow that can either heat the buildings, charge the heat tank, or be turned into cooling through the AC; and every kWh imported from the grid can either serve the electric load, charge an EV, or drive the EC. That is the essence of multi-energy optimisation.

Mathematical formulation

New decision variables

  • $H_{\text{ac}}(t) \ge 0$: heat input drawn from the heat bus by the absorption chiller.
  • $P_{\text{ec}}(t) \ge 0$: electricity input drawn from the electric bus by the electric chiller.

The cooling output of each device is its input multiplied by its COP; we never need to introduce the cooling outputs as variables because the cooling balance pins them.

Three coupled balances

Electric balance (the previous one with one extra term):

$$ \begin{aligned} & P_{\text{dg1}}(t) - P_{\text{ec}}(t) + P_{\text{dg2}}(t) + R_{\text{dg1}}(t) + P_{\text{chp1}}(t) + P_{\text{chp2}}(t) \\ & \;\; + B_{\text{dchg}}(t) - B_{\text{chg}}(t) + \sum_{n} \big( P_{\text{ev,dchg}}^{n}(t) - P_{\text{ev,chg}}^{n}(t) \big) + P_{\text{Gbuy}}(t) - P_{\text{Gsell}}(t) = P_{\text{load}}(t). \end{aligned} $$

Heat balance (the previous one with one extra term):

$$ H_{\text{hob}}(t) - H_{\text{ac}}(t) + H_{\text{chp1}}(t) + H_{\text{chp2}}(t) + H_{\text{Gbuy}}(t) - H_{\text{Gsell}}(t) - H_{\text{chg}}(t) + H_{\text{dchg}}(t) = H_{\text{load}}(t). $$

Cooling balance (new):

$$ \text{COP}_{\text{ac}}\, H_{\text{ac}}(t) + \text{COP}_{\text{ec}}\, P_{\text{ec}}(t) = C_{\text{load}}(t). $$

The values used in the implementation are $\text{COP}_{\text{ac}} = 0.85$ and $\text{COP}_{\text{ec}} = 0.95$. Both COPs are below one because the inputs are measured in primary energy on each carrier.

Big-M mutual exclusion

All storage devices (battery, heat tank, every EV) reuse the Big-M selectors from the previous posts, with $M = 10^{6}$ (written 1e6 in the C++ code):

$$ B_{\text{chg}}(t) \le M\, u_{\text{bat}}(t),\; B_{\text{dchg}}(t) \le M\,(1 - u_{\text{bat}}(t)), $$ $$ H_{\text{chg}}(t) \le M\, u_{\text{hst}}(t),\; H_{\text{dchg}}(t) \le M\,(1 - u_{\text{hst}}(t)), $$ $$ P_{\text{ev,chg}}^{n}(t) \le M\, u_{\text{ev}}^{n}(t),\; P_{\text{ev,dchg}}^{n}(t) \le M\,(1 - u_{\text{ev}}^{n}(t)). $$

Why this matters

The cooling balance is what finally couples all three carriers into a single optimisation: the AC creates a substitution between the heat bus and the cooling bus, the EC between the electric bus and the cooling bus, and the CHPs between the electric bus and the heat bus. The optimal dispatch can therefore arbitrage in non-obvious ways — for instance, run a CHP harder than its electric load justifies because its co-produced heat is cheaper to convert into cooling through the AC than to buy electricity to drive the EC.

Implementation in C++/CPLEX

The complete code lives in trk-blogs/p4/main.cpp. The cooling extension is small enough that we can present it in four pieces.

Cooling demand

int* Cload  = new int[T] {
    100, 100,  80, 100, 120, 135, 150, 135,
    125, 130, 140, 150, 150, 130, 120, 110,
     90,  80, 135, 150, 135, 140, 110, 125 };

Two new decision variables

// Hac : heat drawn from the heat bus by the absorption chiller (kWh-th)
// Pec : electricity drawn from the electric bus by the electric chiller (kW)
IloNumVarArray Hac (env, T, 0, IloInfinity);
IloNumVarArray Pec (env, T, 0, IloInfinity);

// Big-M mutex selectors (battery, heat storage; per-EV uev declared below)
IloBoolVarArray ubat(env, T);
IloBoolVarArray uhst(env, T);

// EV decision variables (same as p3) plus per-vehicle Big-M selector uev
NumVar2D  Pevchg   (env, numEvs);
NumVar2D  Pevdischg(env, numEvs);
NumVar2D  evsoc    (env, numEvs);
BoolVar2D uev      (env, numEvs);
for (int n = 0; n < numEvs; n++) {
    Pevchg[n]    = IloNumVarArray (env, T, 0, evcap[n], ILOFLOAT);
    Pevdischg[n] = IloNumVarArray (env, T, 0, evcap[n], ILOFLOAT);
    evsoc[n]     = IloNumVarArray (env, T, 0, 1,        ILOFLOAT);
    uev[n]       = IloBoolVarArray(env, T);
}

The three coupled balances

for (int t = 0; t < T; t++)
{
    IloExpr evCharge   (env);
    IloExpr evDischarge(env);
    for (int n = 0; n < numEvs; n++) {
        evCharge    += Pevchg[n][t];
        evDischarge += Pevdischg[n][t];
    }

    // Electric balance — Pec is a new load.
    model.add(Pdg1[t] - Pec[t] + Pdg2[t] + Rdg1[t]
              + Pchp1[t] + Pchp2[t]
              + Bdischg[t] - Bchg[t]
              + evDischarge - evCharge
              + PGbuy[t] - PGsell[t] == Pload[t]);

    // Heat balance — Hac is a new load on the heat bus.
    model.add(Hhob[t] - Hac[t] + Hchp1[t] + Hchp2[t]
              + HGbuy[t] - HGsell[t]
              - Hchg[t]  + Hdischg[t] == Hload[t]);

    // Cooling balance — combined chiller output meets the cooling demand.
    model.add(0.85 * Hac[t] + 0.95 * Pec[t] == Cload[t]);
}

Results

Solving the program produces a 24-hour multi-carrier dispatch in which the AC and the EC together cover the cooling demand at every hour. The share between them shifts with the hourly prices: heat-driven cooling dominates when the CHPs run with surplus heat or when district-heat prices are low; electric cooling dominates during midday hours when the PV plant injects free electricity that the chiller can absorb.

Closing the series

This four-post series went from the smallest possible MILP — a 24-hour electricity-only dispatch — to a three-carrier model with EVs and chillers in a few incremental steps. The skeleton has not changed: a fixed horizon, a small set of decision variables per hour, a single IloMinimize objective, and one balance equation per carrier plus the inter-carrier coupling devices. From here, the natural follow-ups are reserve markets, start-up costs and minimum up/down times for the dispatchable generators, robust or stochastic counterparts of the same MILP, and finally the move from a single microgrid to a network of them.
