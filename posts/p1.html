Most introductory optimisation tutorials stop at toy examples with two variables and a single constraint. They are useful to make sense of the abstract form of a linear program, but the gap between such a toy and the first real engineering problem you actually want to solve can feel wide. This post is the bridge I wish I had when I started: a complete, runnable C++/CPLEX program that solves a 24-hour economic dispatch of a small microgrid, with every modelling choice explained alongside the code.

The series is written with two audiences in mind. First, researchers and engineers who are just stepping into optimisation for energy systems and want a single landing mark that ties the theory (decision variables, objective, constraints, Big-M reformulations) to the code that actually solves it. Despite the advent of large language models that have made writing code dramatically easier, the underlying modelling judgement — what to declare as a variable, where to bind a constraint, why a Big-M is needed at all — still has to be learned, and is much easier to internalise from a worked example than from an API reference. Second, the same end-to-end posts (problem statement, formulation, C++ code, results) are designed to be reusable as few-shot training and benchmarking material for LLMs on multi-energy optimisation tasks, where each post is a self-contained prompt–solution pair grounded in a real, runnable model.

The problem

Consider a microgrid that, over a horizon of $T = 24$ hours, must serve an electrical load $P_{\text{load}}(t)$ using four kinds of resources:

  • Two dispatchable generators (DG-1 and DG-2) with per-kW operating costs $C_{\text{dg1}}$ and $C_{\text{dg2}}$ and capacity limits.
  • Two non-dispatchable PV units with hourly forecasts $R_{\text{dg1}}(t)$ and $R_{\text{dg2}}(t)$.
  • A battery storage system characterised by capacity $P_{\text{bmax}}$, round-trip efficiency $\eta$, and an initial state of charge $\text{SoC}_{\text{ini}}$.
  • A connection to the upstream grid where energy can be bought at price $C_{\text{Gbuy}}(t)$ or sold back at $C_{\text{Gsell}}(t)$.

The goal is to choose, for every hour, how much each generator produces, how the battery is charged or discharged, and how much energy is exchanged with the grid, so as to minimise the total operating cost while meeting demand.

Mathematical formulation

Decision variables

For each hour $t \in \{1, \dots, T\}$:

  • $P_{\text{dg1}}(t),\, P_{\text{dg2}}(t) \ge 0$: dispatchable generator outputs.
  • $P_{\text{Gbuy}}(t),\, P_{\text{Gsell}}(t) \ge 0$: power bought from / sold to the grid.
  • $B_{\text{chg}}(t),\, B_{\text{dchg}}(t) \ge 0$: battery charging / discharging power.
  • $u_{\text{bat}}(t) \in \{0,1\}$: battery mode indicator ($1$ when the battery is allowed to charge, $0$ when it is allowed to discharge).
  • $\text{SoC}(t) \in [\text{SoC}_{\min}, \text{SoC}_{\max}]$: battery state of charge, with $\text{SoC}_{\min} = 0.1$ and $\text{SoC}_{\max} = 0.9$.

Objective

Minimise the total operating cost over the horizon:

$$ \min \; \sum_{t=1}^{T} \Big[ C_{\text{dg1}}\, P_{\text{dg1}}(t) + C_{\text{dg2}}\, P_{\text{dg2}}(t) + C_{\text{Gbuy}}(t)\, P_{\text{Gbuy}}(t) - C_{\text{Gsell}}(t)\, P_{\text{Gsell}}(t) \Big]. $$

Constraints

Power balance. For every hour the supply must equal the demand:

$$ P_{\text{dg1}}(t) + P_{\text{dg2}}(t) + R_{\text{dg1}}(t) + R_{\text{dg2}}(t) + B_{\text{dchg}}(t) - B_{\text{chg}}(t) + P_{\text{Gbuy}}(t) - P_{\text{Gsell}}(t) = P_{\text{load}}(t). $$

Battery dynamics. The state of charge evolves as

$$ \text{SoC}(t) = \text{SoC}(t-1) + \frac{\eta\, B_{\text{chg}}(t) - B_{\text{dchg}}(t)/\eta}{P_{\text{bmax}}}, $$

with $\text{SoC}(0) = \text{SoC}_{\text{ini}}$, and the charge / discharge powers are bounded so that $\text{SoC}(t)$ stays inside $[\text{SoC}_{\min}, \text{SoC}_{\max}] = [0.1, 0.9]$. Concretely:

$$ B_{\text{chg}}(t) \le \frac{P_{\text{bmax}}\,(\text{SoC}_{\max} - \text{SoC}(t-1))}{\eta}, \quad B_{\text{dchg}}(t) \le P_{\text{bmax}}\,(\text{SoC}(t-1) - \text{SoC}_{\min})\,\eta. $$

No simultaneous charge and discharge. Without an additional constraint, a purely continuous formulation can charge and discharge the battery in the same hour, which is physically meaningless and only happens in the model because the round-trip efficiency leaks energy. We forbid it through a binary mode indicator $u_{\text{bat}}(t) \in \{0,1\}$ and a Big-M formulation:

$$ B_{\text{chg}}(t) \le M_{\text{chg}}\, u_{\text{bat}}(t), \qquad B_{\text{dchg}}(t) \le M_{\text{dis}}\, \bigl(1 - u_{\text{bat}}(t)\bigr). $$

When $u_{\text{bat}}(t) = 1$ the discharging power is forced to zero; when $u_{\text{bat}}(t) = 0$ the charging power is forced to zero. We use a single large constant $M_{\text{chg}} = M_{\text{dis}} = 10^{6}$ (written 1e6 in the C++ code) so that the binary indicator, rather than the Big-M itself, is what fixes one side to zero. The separate SoC-derived charge / discharge bounds keep the model tight in practice. With this binary variable in place the problem is a mixed-integer linear program (MILP), which CPLEX solves with its branch-and-cut engine.

Implementation in C++/CPLEX

The complete code lives in trk-blogs/p1/main.cpp. We walk through it in six small pieces.

1. Input data

We hard-code the 24-hour profiles for load, prices, and PV forecasts in plain C arrays. A real workflow would read these from a CSV or a database; keeping them inline makes the example self-contained. 

int T     = 24;   // One day (hours)
int Cdg1  = 80;   // Cost per kW from DG-1
int Cdg2  = 90;   // Cost per kW from DG-2

int* Pload = new int[T] {
    169, 175, 179, 171, 181, 172, 270, 264,
    273, 281, 193, 158, 161, 162, 250, 260,
    267, 271, 284, 167, 128, 134, 144, 150 };

int* CGbuy = new int[T] {
     90,  90,  90,  90,  90,  90, 110, 110,
    110, 110, 110, 125, 125, 125, 125, 125,
    125, 125, 110, 110, 110, 110, 110, 110 };

int* CGsell = new int[T] {
     70,  70,  70,  70,  70,  70,  90,  90,
     90,  90,  90, 105, 105, 105, 105, 105,
    105, 105,  90,  90,  90,  90,  90,  90 };

float* Rdg1 = new float[T] {
    0,0,0,0,0,0,0,10,15,20,23,28,
    33,35,34,31,28,10,0,0,0,0,0,0 };

float* Rdg2 = new float[T] {
    0,0,0,0,0,0,0,10,15,20,23,28,
    33,35,34,31,28,10,0,0,0,0,0,0 };

float socini = 0.2f, socmin = 0.1f, socmax = 0.9f;  // Battery SoC: initial, min, max
int   Pbmax  = 200;    // Battery capacity (kWh)
float effin  = 0.95f;  // Battery efficiency

2. Decision variables

CPLEX exposes IloNumVarArray for vectors of continuous variables and IloBoolVarArray for vectors of binaries. We pass the lower and upper bounds directly at construction; this is equivalent to adding two box constraints and produces a slightly leaner model. 

const float Mchg = 1e6f; // Big-M for charging power
const float Mdis = 1e6f; // Big-M for discharging power

IloNumVarArray  PGbuy  (env, T, 0, IloInfinity); // Grid power bought
IloNumVarArray  PGsell (env, T, 0, IloInfinity); // Grid power sold

IloNumVarArray  statoc (env, T, socmin, socmax);    // Battery state of charge (constrained to [socmin, socmax])
IloNumVarArray  Bchg   (env, T, 0, Mchg); // Battery charging power
IloNumVarArray  Bdischg(env, T, 0, Mdis); // Battery discharging power
IloBoolVarArray ubat   (env, T);          // 1 = charging, 0 = discharging

IloNumVarArray  Pdg1   (env, T, 0,  80);  // DG-1 output
IloNumVarArray  Pdg2   (env, T, 0, 100);  // DG-2 output

3. Objective

The objective is built incrementally with an IloExpr. Selling to the grid appears with a negative sign because revenue offsets cost. 

IloExpr objective(env);
for (int t = 0; t < T; t++)
{
    objective += Cdg1 * Pdg1[t]
               + Cdg2 * Pdg2[t]
               + CGbuy[t]  * PGbuy[t]
               - CGsell[t] * PGsell[t];
}
model.add(IloMinimize(env, objective));

4. Constraints

Every constraint is added inside a single loop over $t$. The first hour is treated as a special case so the SoC dynamics can refer to $\text{SoC}_{\text{ini}}$ instead of $\text{SoC}(t-1)$. Note in particular the two Big-M inequalities that link $B_{\text{chg}}$ and $B_{\text{dchg}}$ to the binary mode indicator $u_{\text{bat}}$. 

for (int t = 0; t < T; t++)
{
    // DG output bounds
    model.add(Pdg1[t] <= 80);
    model.add(Pdg2[t] <= 90);

    // Mutual exclusion: charge XOR discharge via Big-M and binary ubat
    model.add(Bchg[t]    <= Mchg *      ubat[t]);
    model.add(Bdischg[t] <= Mdis * (1 - ubat[t]));

    // Battery dynamics and SoC-derived charge / discharge limits
    if (t == 0)
    {
        model.add(statoc[t] == socini
                  + ((effin * Bchg[t] - (Bdischg[t] / effin)) / Pbmax));
        model.add(Bchg[t]    <= (Pbmax * (socmax - socini) / effin));
        model.add(Bdischg[t] <= (Pbmax * (socini - socmin) * effin));
    }
    else
    {
        model.add(statoc[t] == statoc[t - 1]
                  + ((effin * Bchg[t] - (Bdischg[t] / effin)) / Pbmax));
        model.add(Bchg[t]    <= (Pbmax * (socmax - statoc[t - 1])) / effin);
        model.add(Bdischg[t] <= Pbmax * (statoc[t - 1] - socmin) * effin);
    }

    // Power balance
    model.add(Pdg1[t] + Pdg2[t] + Rdg1[t] + Rdg2[t]
              + Bdischg[t] - Bchg[t]
              + PGbuy[t]   - PGsell[t] == Pload[t]);
}

5. Solving and reporting

IloCplex extracts the model, optionally exports it to a CPLEX .lp file for inspection, and solves it. We always check the return value of cplex.solve() before reading any value back: a model that is infeasible or unbounded will not have a meaningful objective. 

IloCplex cplex(env);
cplex.extract(model);
cplex.exportModel("Model.lp");
cplex.setOut(env.getNullStream());

if (!cplex.solve()) {
    env.error() << "Failed" << endl;
    throw(-1);
}

double obj = cplex.getObjValue();
auto end   = chrono::high_resolution_clock::now();
auto ms    = chrono::duration_cast<chrono::milliseconds>(end - start);

cout << "\n\tElapsed time (ms): " << ms.count()  << endl;
cout << "Solution status     : " << cplex.getStatus() << endl;
cout << "Minimized objective : " << obj << endl;

6. Saving results to CSV

Finally we dump the optimal trajectories of every variable to output.csv so they can be plotted or post-processed. 

std::ofstream outputFile("output.csv");
if (outputFile.is_open()) {
    outputFile << "Time,Pload,CGbuy,CGsell,Rdg1,Rdg2,"
                  "PGbuy,PGsell,statoc,Bchg,Bdischg,ubat,Pdg1,Pdg2"
               << std::endl;

    for (int i = 0; i < T; i++) {
        outputFile << i + 1                 << ","
                   << Pload[i]              << ","
                   << CGbuy[i]              << ","
                   << CGsell[i]             << ","
                   << Rdg1[i]               << ","
                   << Rdg2[i]               << ","
                   <<  cplex.getValue(PGbuy[i])   << ","
                   << -cplex.getValue(PGsell[i])  << ","
                   <<  cplex.getValue(statoc[i])  << ","
                   << -cplex.getValue(Bchg[i])    << ","
                   <<  cplex.getValue(Bdischg[i]) << ","
                   <<  cplex.getValue(ubat[i])    << ","
                   <<  cplex.getValue(Pdg1[i])    << ","
                   <<  cplex.getValue(Pdg2[i])    << std::endl;
    }
    outputFile.close();
}

Results

Solving the program with the input data above yields a 24-hour dispatch plan that uses the cheapest generator (DG-1) at full capacity throughout the horizon, fills in the demand gap with DG-2 during the morning peak, charges the battery from the grid when prices are low, and sells back during the high-price midday window. With the new Big-M constraint the battery is never charged and discharged in the same hour, as can be seen from the $u_{\text{bat}}$ column — rows where charging happens have $u_{\text{bat}} = 1$ and zero $B_{\text{dchg}}$, and vice versa for discharging hours. Where to go next

This formulation is intentionally minimal so that every decision and constraint is easy to inspect. Natural extensions include adding binary on/off variables for the dispatchable generators, modelling start-up costs and minimum up/down times, introducing reserve requirements, and accounting for forecast uncertainty through robust or stochastic formulations.

The next three posts of this series build on exactly this skeleton:

  • Post 2 — From Single Energy Microgrid to Multi Energy: adds the heat sector (CHPs, a heat-only boiler, a heat tank, and bidirectional district-heat trading) on top of the electric layer, with a second balance equation and the CHP heat-to-power coupling.
  • Post 3 — Adding Electric Vehicles as Flexible Storage: introduces a fleet of plug-in EVs as time-windowed storage on the electric bus, each with its own arrival, departure, capacity and a minimum departure SoC.
  • Post 4 — Closing the Loop with Cooling: adds the cooling sector via an absorption chiller and an electric chiller, finally coupling all three energy carriers (electricity, heat, cooling) into a single MILP.
