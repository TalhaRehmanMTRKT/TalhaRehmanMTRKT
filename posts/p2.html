In the previous post we wrote a small C++/CPLEX program that solved the 24-hour economic dispatch of an electricity-only microgrid: two dispatchable generators, two PV units, a battery, and a grid tie. The goal was to minimise operating cost subject to a single power-balance equation per hour, with a Big-M binary forbidding simultaneous charging and discharging of the battery.

Real systems rarely live on a single energy carrier. Buildings need heat, data centres need cooling, and modern microgrids couple all of these through devices that translate one carrier into another. This post introduces the heat sector, which is the simplest non-trivial multi-energy extension and the foundation everything else in this series will build on.

Recap and what changes

The new ingredients are:

  • A heat-only boiler (HOB) that burns fuel to produce heat.
  • Two combined heat and power units (CHP-1, CHP-2) that produce electricity and heat simultaneously, with a fixed heat-to-power ratio $k_i$.
  • A heat storage tank with its own state of charge, round-trip efficiency, and capacity.
  • A bidirectional connection to a district heat network, with hourly buying and selling prices.

The electric layer (DGs, PV, battery, grid tie) is unchanged in spirit; the equations from the previous post still hold. We add a second balance equation for heat, and a coupling constraint between each CHP's electric and heat outputs.

Mathematical formulation

Decision variables

We keep all variables from the previous post and add, for each hour $t \in \{1,\dots,T\}$:

  • $P_{\text{chp1}}(t),\, P_{\text{chp2}}(t)$: CHP electric outputs, bounded between a minimum and a maximum operating point.
  • $H_{\text{chp1}}(t),\, H_{\text{chp2}}(t)$: CHP heat outputs.
  • $H_{\text{hob}}(t)$: heat output of the heat-only boiler.
  • $H_{\text{Gbuy}}(t),\, H_{\text{Gsell}}(t) \ge 0$: heat bought from / sold to the district network.
  • $H_{\text{chg}}(t),\, H_{\text{dchg}}(t) \ge 0$: heat-storage charging / discharging power.
  • $\text{HSSsoc}(t) \in [0,1]$: heat-storage state of charge.
  • $u_{\text{hst}}(t) \in \{0,1\}$: heat-storage charge / discharge selector.

Objective

The objective gains explicit operating-cost terms for the new generators and a heat-trading term mirroring the electric one:

$$ \min \sum_{t=1}^{T} \Big[ C_{\text{dg1}} P_{\text{dg1}}(t) + C_{\text{dg2}} P_{\text{dg2}}(t) + C_{\text{chp1}} P_{\text{chp1}}(t) + C_{\text{chp2}} P_{\text{chp2}}(t) + C_{\text{hob}} H_{\text{hob}}(t) $$ $$ +\, C_{\text{Gbuy}}(t) P_{\text{Gbuy}}(t) - C_{\text{Gsell}}(t) P_{\text{Gsell}}(t) + C_{\text{Hbuy}}(t) H_{\text{Gbuy}}(t) - C_{\text{Hsell}}(t) H_{\text{Gsell}}(t) \Big]. $$

Note that the CHPs are paid only for their electric output: the co-produced heat is a free by-product of the same fuel burn, an assumption that already makes them economically attractive on a heat-loaded hour.

Coupling and balance constraints

CHP heat-to-power coupling. A back-pressure CHP cannot vary its heat and power independently. We use the simplest possible model, a fixed ratio $k_i$:

$$ H_{\text{chp},i}(t) = k_i\, P_{\text{chp},i}(t), \qquad i \in \{1,2\}. $$

Heat balance. An additional balance equation closes the heat sector:

$$ H_{\text{hob}}(t) + H_{\text{chp1}}(t) + H_{\text{chp2}}(t) + H_{\text{Gbuy}}(t) - H_{\text{Gsell}}(t) + H_{\text{dchg}}(t) - H_{\text{chg}}(t) = H_{\text{load}}(t). $$

Heat-storage dynamics. Mirror the battery: with efficiency $\eta_H$ and capacity $H_{\text{ss,max}}$,

$$ \text{HSSsoc}(t) = \text{HSSsoc}(t-1) + \frac{\eta_H\, H_{\text{chg}}(t) - H_{\text{dchg}}(t)/\eta_H}{H_{\text{ss,max}}}, $$

with $\text{HSSsoc}(0) = 0.5$ for this example.

Mutual exclusion of charge and discharge. A purely linear relaxation would happily charge and discharge a storage at the same time to game round-trip losses. As in the previous post we forbid this with a Big-M binary, now applied to both storage units:

$$ B_{\text{chg}}(t) \le M\, u_{\text{bat}}(t), \quad B_{\text{dchg}}(t) \le M\,(1 - u_{\text{bat}}(t)), $$ $$ H_{\text{chg}}(t) \le M\, u_{\text{hst}}(t), \quad H_{\text{dchg}}(t) \le M\,(1 - u_{\text{hst}}(t)), $$

with $u_{\text{bat}}(t), u_{\text{hst}}(t) \in \{0,1\}$ and $M = 10^{6}$ (written 1e6 in the C++ code).

Power balance. The electric balance gains the CHP injections but is otherwise unchanged from the previous post:

$$ P_{\text{dg1}}(t) + P_{\text{dg2}}(t) + R_{\text{dg1}}(t) + P_{\text{chp1}}(t) + P_{\text{chp2}}(t) + B_{\text{dchg}}(t) - B_{\text{chg}}(t) + P_{\text{Gbuy}}(t) - P_{\text{Gsell}}(t) = P_{\text{load}}(t). $$

Implementation in C++/CPLEX

The complete code lives in trk-blogs/p2/main.cpp. It follows the same skeleton as the previous post; we walk only through the new pieces.

Input data

We add hourly heat-demand and heat-trading-price arrays, plus the parameters of the heat sector (HOB and CHP costs, heat-storage capacity and efficiency, the CHP heat-to-power ratios $k_1, k_2$).

// Generation costs (per kW)
int Cdg1  = 135;
int Cdg2  = 140;
int Chob  = 80;
int Cchp1 = 150;
int Cchp2 = 145;

// Storage parameters
float socini = 0.2f;   // Initial battery SoC
int   Pbmax  = 100;    // Battery capacity (kWh)
float effin  = 0.95f;  // Battery efficiency
int   Hssmax = 50;     // Heat-storage capacity (kWh-th)
float Heffin = 0.95f;  // Heat-storage efficiency

// CHP heat-to-electric ratios
float k1 = 1.2f;
float k2 = 0.8f;

// Demands, trading prices, PV forecast: 24-hour arrays (see main.cpp).

Decision variables

The electric-side variables are the same as before. The heat side adds the CHP, HOB, heat-grid and heat-storage variables, plus the two binary selectors $u_{\text{bat}}, u_{\text{hst}}$ used below to enforce charge / discharge mutual exclusion.

// Electric side
IloNumVarArray  PGbuy  (env, T, 0, IloInfinity);
IloNumVarArray  PGsell (env, T, 0, IloInfinity);
IloNumVarArray  statoc (env, T, 0, 1);
IloNumVarArray  Bchg   (env, T, 0, 50);
IloNumVarArray  Bdischg(env, T, 0, 50);
IloBoolVarArray ubat   (env, T);              // 1 = charge, 0 = discharge
IloNumVarArray  Pdg1   (env, T, 0, 100, ILOINT);
IloNumVarArray  Pdg2   (env, T, 0,  80, ILOINT);
IloNumVarArray  Pchp1  (env, T, 30, 60, ILOINT);
IloNumVarArray  Pchp2  (env, T, 50,100, ILOINT);

// Heat side
IloNumVarArray  HGbuy  (env, T, 0, IloInfinity, ILOINT);
IloNumVarArray  HGsell (env, T, 0, IloInfinity, ILOINT);
IloNumVarArray  HSSsoc (env, T, 0, 1);
IloNumVarArray  Hchg   (env, T, 0, 50, ILOINT);
IloNumVarArray  Hdischg(env, T, 0, 50, ILOINT);
IloBoolVarArray uhst   (env, T);              // 1 = charge, 0 = discharge
IloNumVarArray  Hhob   (env, T, 0, 80, ILOINT);
IloNumVarArray  Hchp1  (env, T, 0, IloInfinity);
IloNumVarArray  Hchp2  (env, T, 0, IloInfinity);

Objective

IloExpr objective(env);
for (int t = 0; t < T; t++)
{
    objective += Cdg1  * Pdg1[t]  + Cdg2  * Pdg2[t]
               + Cchp1 * Pchp1[t] + Cchp2 * Pchp2[t]
               + Chob  * Hhob[t]
               + CGbuy[t] * PGbuy[t] - CGsell[t] * PGsell[t]
               + CHbuy[t] * HGbuy[t] - CHsell[t] * HGsell[t];
}
model.add(IloMinimize(env, objective));

Constraints

The new constraints split naturally in three blocks: device bounds and the CHP heat-to-power coupling; storage dynamics for both the electric battery and the heat tank; and the two balance equations.

for (int t = 0; t < T; t++)
{
    // Dispatchable-generator output bounds
    model.add(Pdg1[t] <= 100);
    model.add(Pdg2[t] <=  80);

    // Heat-only boiler bound
    model.add(Hhob[t] <= 80);

    // CHP electric output bands (must be on, between min and max)
    model.add(Pchp1[t] >= 30); model.add(Pchp1[t] <= 60);
    model.add(Pchp2[t] >= 50); model.add(Pchp2[t] <= 100);

    // CHP heat output is locked to its electric output through a fixed ratio
    model.add(Hchp1[t] == k1 * Pchp1[t]);
    model.add(Hchp2[t] == k2 * Pchp2[t]);
}

for (int t = 0; t < T; t++)
{
    // Big-M mutual exclusion: charge XOR discharge for each storage unit
    model.add(Bchg[t]    <= 1e6 *      ubat[t]);
    model.add(Bdischg[t] <= 1e6 * (1 - ubat[t]));
    model.add(Hchg[t]    <= 1e6 *      uhst[t]);
    model.add(Hdischg[t] <= 1e6 * (1 - uhst[t]));

    if (t == 0) {
        model.add(statoc[t] == socini
                  + ((effin * Bchg[t] - Bdischg[t] / effin) / Pbmax));
        model.add(Bchg[t]    <= (Pbmax * (1 - socini) / effin));
        model.add(Bdischg[t] <= (Pbmax * socini * effin));

        model.add(HSSsoc[t] == 0.5
                  + ((Heffin * Hchg[t] - Hdischg[t] / Heffin) / Hssmax));
        model.add(Hchg[t]    <= (Hssmax * 0.5 / Heffin));
        model.add(Hdischg[t] <= (Hssmax * 0.5 * Heffin));
    } else {
        model.add(statoc[t] == statoc[t - 1]
                  + ((effin * Bchg[t] - Bdischg[t] / effin) / Pbmax));
        model.add(Bchg[t]    <= (Pbmax * (1 - statoc[t - 1])) / effin);
        model.add(Bdischg[t] <= Pbmax * statoc[t - 1] * effin);

        model.add(HSSsoc[t] == HSSsoc[t - 1]
                  + ((Heffin * Hchg[t] - Hdischg[t] / Heffin) / Hssmax));
        model.add(Hchg[t]    <= (Hssmax * (1 - HSSsoc[t - 1]) / Heffin));
        model.add(Hdischg[t] <= Hssmax * HSSsoc[t - 1] * Heffin);
    }
}

for (int t = 0; t < T; t++)
{
    // Electric balance
    model.add(Pdg1[t] + Pdg2[t] + Rdg1[t]
              + Pchp1[t] + Pchp2[t]
              + Bdischg[t] - Bchg[t]
              + PGbuy[t]   - PGsell[t] == Pload[t]);

    // Heat balance
    model.add(Hhob[t] + Hchp1[t] + Hchp2[t]
              + HGbuy[t] - HGsell[t]
              - Hchg[t]  + Hdischg[t] == Hload[t]);
}

Results

Solving the program with the input data above produces a 24-hour co-dispatch in which the CHPs run close to their economic optimum, covering most of the heat demand directly, while the HOB and the heat storage absorb the residual mismatch. The electric balance then uses the cheapest combination of DG output, PV, CHP electric by-product, and grid exchange.

Where to go next

A natural next step is to bring Electric Vehicles into the electric layer. Their arrival and departure times turn the EV fleet into a time-windowed flexible storage that is simultaneously richer and harder to schedule than a fixed battery. We add it in the next post; the rest of the model — heat sector, storage dynamics, and the two balance equations introduced here — will be inherited as is.

The remaining posts in the series build directly on this multi-energy skeleton:

  • Post 3 — Adding Electric Vehicles as Flexible Storage: a fleet of plug-in EVs is modelled as 2D decision variables (time × vehicle) with arrival, departure, capacity and a minimum departure SoC, plus a per-EV Big-M mutex on charge / discharge.
  • Post 4 — Closing the Loop with Cooling: an absorption chiller and an electric chiller add a third balance equation, finally coupling electricity, heat and cooling in a single MILP.
